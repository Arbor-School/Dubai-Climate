🌡 What Is Climate Change?

Climate change is the long-term warming of Earth's climate system, mainly caused by extra greenhouse gases from human activities like burning fossil fuels and deforestation.

  • Greenhouse gases such as carbon dioxide, methane, and nitrous oxide trap more heat in the atmosphere.
  • Burning oil and gas for electricity, cooling, and transport releases large amounts of these gases.
  • Deforestation and land use changes remove plants that normally absorb carbon dioxide.
  • As the planet warms, we see higher temperatures, sea level rise, stronger heat waves, and shifting rainfall patterns.

🏙 Human Impacts in Al Furjan & Dubai

Al Furjan is part of a rapidly growing area of Dubai, with new residential communities, roads, and infrastructure that increase energy use and emissions.

Dubai's growth, desalination plants, artificial islands, and high demand for cooling place extra pressure on the Gulf's water, marine life, and climate.

🏗

Urban expansion

Construction of new communities, towers, and roads replaces natural desert habitats and increases heat stored by buildings and asphalt.

🌴

Artificial islands

The Palm and other land-reclamation projects change currents and coastal water temperatures, putting stress on coral reefs and marine life.

❄️

High energy use

Air conditioning is essential in Dubai's extreme heat, but it increases electricity demand and emissions if powered by fossil fuels.

🚗

Transport emissions

Cars, trucks, and planes emit carbon dioxide and other pollutants, although public transport and greener mobility strategies help cut emissions.

🏖

Tourism & lifestyle

Tourism, malls, and attractions use large amounts of energy and water but also create chances to showcase sustainable design and green building.

💧

Water desalination

Dubai relies on desalination, which is energy-intensive and produces salty brine that can harm marine ecosystems if not managed carefully.

Dubai has invested in more efficient power and water production, cutting millions of tonnes of carbon dioxide emissions by improving plant efficiency.

🐠 Effects on Local Ecosystems

Climate change and local human activities affect coral reefs, marine life, mangroves, and desert species around Dubai.

  • Coral reefs are stressed by warmer waters and changes in water quality, which can lead to coral bleaching and biodiversity loss.
  • Mangroves sequester carbon, protect the coast, and support fish nurseries, but sea-level rise and coastal development can damage these forests.
  • Desert wildlife such as camels, reptiles, and birds face higher heat stress and less available water as temperatures rise.
  • Seagrass beds and marine ecosystems are sensitive to changes in salinity and temperature from desalination and warming.

🏫 The Arbor School & Sustainability Policies

The Arbor School in Al Furjan is an ecological British-curriculum school built on the three pillars of ecoliteracy, sustainability, and environmental justice.

Its vision "Enough for all, forever" guides policies that encourage students to understand and act on local and global climate challenges.

🧠

Ecoliteracy framework

The curriculum integrates ecoliteracy across subjects, helping students analyse biodiversity, climate justice, and sustainable development from Key Stage 3 to Sixth Form.

🌍

"Head, Heart, Hands, Spirit"

Arbor's approach develops scientific understanding, emotional connection, practical action, and reflective purpose in environmental learning.

♻️

School-wide policies

Policies promote reduced waste, careful energy and water use, sustainable transport choices, and community projects linked to local ecosystems.

  • Students learn in biodomes, gardens, and "living classrooms" that connect lessons to real ecosystems.
  • Secondary students study Global Perspectives and eco-focused projects that link directly to climate and sustainability issues.
  • Partnerships and events such as eco-walks and coastal activities link school learning to wider community action.

By embedding ecoliteracy into the curriculum, Arbor's policies aim to ensure students leave school with both the knowledge and willingness to take environmental action.

🏜 What Dubai Can Do

Dubai is already launching major initiatives to tackle climate change, and there are more actions the city can take to protect areas like Al Furjan and the Gulf coast.

  • Expand mangrove restoration: Projects to plant 100 million mangrove trees along 72 km of coastline could store over 1 million tonnes of CO₂ each year and protect coasts from storms.
  • Increase clean energy: Building more solar farms and phasing out fossil fuels in power plants would cut emissions from electricity and water desalination.
  • Greener buildings & cooling: Designing highly efficient buildings, using better insulation, district cooling, and smart controls can reduce AC energy use in hot districts like Al Furjan.
  • Low-carbon transport: Expanding metro and tram lines, improving buses, safe cycling routes, and encouraging electric vehicles can lower transport emissions.
  • Protect marine habitats: Stronger rules on brine disposal, wastewater, and coastal construction can help coral reefs, seagrass, and fish nurseries recover.
  • Educate and involve residents: Supporting schools such as The Arbor School, citizen science, and community campaigns ensures people understand both problems and solutions.

Dubai's green vision depends on both large projects and everyday choices made by residents, schools, and local communities.

🌱 Solutions & Actions for Students

Every student at The Arbor School can make a difference by changing daily habits, supporting school projects, and sharing what they learn with others.

Choose efficient settings for AC at home, switch off lights and devices when not needed, and close doors and windows to keep cool air in.
Walk, cycle, share rides with family and friends, or use the Metro and other public transport whenever possible.
Take shorter showers, turn off taps while brushing teeth, and report leaks at school or home to reduce pressure on desalination.
Join or start school initiatives such as tree planting, mangrove research, waste reduction campaigns, or energy audits.
Present your findings to classmates, families, and the wider community, and use creative projects to explain local climate issues.

Switching to greener transport and energy can lower emissions from busy urban corridors like those near Al Furjan.

Large city projects matter, but everyday choices by thousands of students also add up to significant climate action over time.
